AI and Jobs: The Final Word (Until the Next One)

Sarah E. Eckhardt, Nathan Goldschlag • 2025 • EIG Report
employment unemployment CPS data AI exposure descriptive

Summary

  • Finds no aggregate effects of generative AI on the labor force in an analysis of Current Population Survey data from 2022 to 2025 using multiple job- and firm-level AI exposure measures.
  • Since 2022, the unemployment rate has risen more for the least AI-exposed workers than for the most.
  • The share of workers exiting the labor force is lowest (and stable) for the most exposed quintile of workers.
  • The most AI-exposed quintile of workers switches occupations more often than less exposed workers, but the switching rate is lower than it was before the Covid pandemic and has not risen since 2022.
  • Unemployment for young workers (22 - 27 year olds) in the most exposed quintile has risen, but less quickly and from lower levels than for young workers in other quintiles.
  • Isolating to the information sector, where businesses are three times more likely than average to say they use AI, employment trends show no break with the introduction of generative AI.
  • Publishing and data processing, the sectors most likely to use AI, have seen roughly flat employment since 2022 after Covid-era surges.

Abstract

Analyzed U.S. Current Population Survey data (2022–2025) using multiple occupation-level AI exposure indices. They find no evidence of AI-driven job losses so far. The unemployment rate rose less for the most AI-exposed quintile of workers (+0.3 pp) than for the least exposed quintile (+0.9 pp) since ChatGPT's release.
